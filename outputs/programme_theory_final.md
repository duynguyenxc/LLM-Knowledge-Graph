### Programme Theory: Enhancing Clinical Reasoning in Novice Medical Students

**What works, for whom, in what respects, to what extent, in what contexts, and how?**

#### Context (C)
The target group for this programme theory consists of novice learners, specifically medical students, who possess 'low knowledge,' limited clinical domain-specific knowledge, or an inability to effectively use knowledge in reasoning situations. These students typically have limited clinical experience and therefore approach most consultations in a more analytical ('hypothetico-deductive') manner.

#### Mechanisms (M)
1. **Mechanism Resource (M1):** The intervention involves teaching resources that allow students to make mistakes. This is operationalized through clinicians demonstrating clinical consultations at normal speed, as they would in regular practice. During these demonstrations, clinicians clearly consider a range of hypotheses and differential diagnoses, providing multiple relevant resources for students to engage with.

2. **Mechanism Response (M2):** Following the demonstration, clinicians deconstruct the consultation with the students. This process is designed to build understanding by assisting students in comprehending the clinician's thought process and reasoning. This reflective practice enables students to internalize the reasoning strategies demonstrated.

#### Outcomes (O)
The primary outcome of this intervention is a positive impact on learning, specifically the development of clinical reasoning skills. By engaging with the teaching resources and participating in the deconstruction of clinical consultations, students are expected to gain a deeper understanding of clinical reasoning processes. This understanding forms the basis for developing a framework for teaching these skills effectively.

#### How it Works
In the context of novice medical students with limited clinical experience, the intervention works by providing a structured learning environment where students can observe and analyze real-time clinical reasoning. The clinician's demonstration serves as a resource that exposes students to the complexities of clinical decision-making. The subsequent deconstruction of the consultation acts as a catalyst for students to reflect on and understand the reasoning process, thereby enhancing their ability to apply similar reasoning in future clinical scenarios.

#### To What Extent
The extent to which this intervention is effective depends on the students' engagement with the process and their ability to reflect on and internalize the reasoning strategies demonstrated. The structured approach of demonstration followed by deconstruction provides a comprehensive framework that supports the development of clinical reasoning skills, potentially leading to significant improvements in students' clinical competencies.

In summary, this programme theory outlines a structured educational approach that leverages real-time clinical demonstrations and reflective deconstruction to enhance clinical reasoning skills in novice medical students. By addressing the specific needs of students with limited clinical experience, this approach facilitates a deeper understanding of clinical reasoning processes, ultimately leading to improved learning outcomes.
