2\. Limitations of Current Systematic Review Methods

For decades, systematic reviews have provided the foundation for addressing education reform, offering methodological rigor through clearly defined protocols and accountable reporting. Yet, the traditional review process faces growing challenges that limit its capacity to meet the demands of rapidly evolving needs in contemporary education. Conventional reviews typically follow a multi-step process: databases are selected, search strings defined, titles and abstracts screened, full texts examined, and data extracted for thematic or narrative synthesis. While this workflow ensures transparency and accountability, it is slow and resource-intensive. Reviews often require months or years to complete, by which time the evidence base may have already shifted. This time lag reduces the usefulness of reviews for informing timely decisions in education (Bond et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024) ). Reviews of human-centered learning analytics have continued to rely on fully manual inclusion and coding, emphasizing rigor at the expense of efficiency (Riordan et al., 2023). Even large-scale mapping reviews have often relied on manual methods, which make it challenging to keep pace with the rapid expansion of publications (Haddaway et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2025). These examples illustrate the trade-off between thoroughness and timeliness. In short, the core strength of existing review methods—their methodological transparency—has become their greatest limitation. Manual approaches preserve rigor but cannot scale to the volume and speed of today’s educational research.



3\. Advances in Current AI Development: Capacity and Reasoning

The limitations of manual review methods underscore the need for approaches that combine rigor with scalability. Recent advances in generative AI and agent-based frameworks make this possible. Multi-agent orchestration frameworks such as LangChain enable AI agents to interact with academic databases, apply rule-based filters, and maintain decision records (Mavroudis, 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Farooq et al., 2024; Seabra et al., 2024; Topsakal et al., 2023). Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) has advanced into graph-based retrieval (GraphRAG), which encodes relationships among educational constructs and principles captured from diverse metadata sources (Han et al., 2024). By mapping evidence as a knowledge graph, GraphRAG detects conceptual patterns and lineages with greater precision (Han et al., 2024). Multi-agent systems extend these capabilities: specialized agents for screening, coding, and synthesis can collaborate and cross-check outputs, improving accuracy while maintaining transparency (Ye et al., 2024). Beyond retrieval, generative AI has gained explicit reasoning mechanisms. Reinforcement learning approaches such as incentivizing multi-step reasoning, yielding strong gains on difficult benchmarks (Guo et al., 2025). Structured inference methods expand beyond chain-of-thought: Tree of Thoughts explores multiple solution branches (Yao et al., 2023), Graph of Thoughts represents reasoning paths as graphs (Besta et al., 2023), and Self-Discover composes new reasoning structures from atomic modules (Zhou et al., 2024). Reflexion is an agent framework that enables iterative self-assessment with episodic memory, allowing AI systems to refine their reasoning across multiple attempts (Shinn et al., 2023). For evidence-based applications, Corrective-RAG dynamically evaluates retrieval quality to improve citation accuracy (Yan et al., 2024), while literature-grounded QA systems demonstrate superhuman-level performance in contradiction detection and citation-grounded synthesis (Skarlinski et al., 2024). By combining retrieval, reasoning, and synthesis, current AI frameworks create the foundation for scalable, auditable, and transparent reviews—precisely what is needed to guide evidence-based educational change.



4\. Proposed AI Agent Pipeline for Systematic Review in Education

Most current AI-assisted review platforms—commercial or research-oriented (e.g., Consensus, Elicit, Scite)—rely heavily on general-purpose language models. While some incorporate retrieval or workflow components, they still lack the critical qualities required for systematic evidence synthesis in education. Their reasoning is opaque, making it impossible to trace claims back to sources (Asai et al., 2023). Retrieval is uncontrolled, driven by ad hoc prompts rather than transparent researcher-defined protocols (O’Connor et al., 2024). Their outputs are unstructured, preventing conversion into reproducible evidence tables or coded datasets (Dagdelen et al., 2024). Most concerning, they smooth over contradictions instead of surfacing them, failing to meet researchers’ needs in clarifying contested findings (Skarlinski et al., 2024). In short, current LLM-based tools can perform literature reviews, but they remain opaque, not directed by researchers, and unable to meet the rigorous and transparent demands of systematic review in education. Although advanced agent-based frameworks for systematic review are beginning to emerge in other domains (Susnjak et al., 2023; Sami et al., 2024; Rouzrokh et al., 2025), no comparable frameworks yet exist for education. To close this gap, we propose an AI agent pipeline purpose-built for systematic reviews in education, designed to meet the rigorous needs specified by this RRE call.



4\.1 Architecture of the AI Agent Pipeline

The proposed pipeline is built on the agentic framework of LangChain, implemented as orchestrated workflows. Within LangChain, multiple agents collaborate under coordinated workflows, each assigned specialized functions such as screening, coding, synthesis, contradiction checks, and citation validation. The orchestration of tasks and information across agents is governed by domain-adapted prompt templates and rule-based decision logic, which comprehensively encode rules and schema definitions for specific review tasks or methods, and can be directly defined and adjusted by researchers to meet their needs. In this way, humans collaborate with AI agents, allowing researchers to guide and shape agent behavior while ensuring reproducibility and alignment with systematic review protocols.



The pipeline integrates two complementary knowledge sources representing domain experts, such as educational researchers. First, a domain-adapted large language model (LLM), fine-tuned on pedagogy and education corpora, provides interpretive reasoning capacity to guide agent decisions. Second, a Literature Knowledge Graph (LKG) serves as an external knowledge base built on the outcomes of different studies. This graph is constructed by extracting entities and relations from publications, where entities correspond to key constructs such as interventions, populations, outcomes, contexts, study designs, and assessments, while relations capture how these constructs are linked (Dagdelen et al., 2024). Citation and co-citation edges linking these entities represent their relationships and further enrich the graph.



Integration of the knowledge graph occurs through Graph-enhanced Retrieval-Augmented Generation (GraphRAG), which extends conventional RAG by retrieving subgraphs of related entities and their relations instead of isolated text passages (Asai et al., 2023; Larson et al. 2025).This enables agents to reason over connected claims and evidence paths, improving conceptual alignment and reducing hallucinations (Yan et al., 2024). In practice, the LangChain agent framework coordinates the interaction between the domain-adapted LLM and the LKG: the LLM controls the flow and interprets results through researcher-defined templates, while the LKG supplies structured, graph-grounded evidence via GraphRAG. Together, they create a transparent and controllable pipeline in which every action—whether the inclusion of a study, the coding of a construct, or the synthesis of findings—is traceable both to the protocol and to a verifiable evidence path.



4\.2 Training and Evaluation Strategy

Training begins with the establishment of the Literature Knowledge Graph. Publications from ERIC, Scopus, Web of Science, and open-access repositories are processed using Named Entity Recognition (NER) and Relation Extraction (RE). These methods automatically identify educational constructs (e.g., intervention, outcome, population) and map their relations (e.g., intervention affects outcome, population measured by assessment). Graph embeddings and link prediction are then used to denoise and complete missing edges, producing a structured knowledge base for retrieval and reasoning (Dagdelen et al., 2024).



Parallel to graph construction, the domain-adapted LLM is fine-tuned on education-specific corpora with self-supervised learning objectives, grounding it in pedagogical discourse (Wu et al., 2024). Once integrated into LangChain, this LLM orchestrates sub-agents under template-based controls, ensuring alignment with the review protocol (Moher et al., 2009). The entire system is then refined through reinforcement learning with human-in-the-loop (RLHF), where human reviewers reward correct screening and coding, penalize unsupported claims, and guide synthesis outputs (Shinn et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Active learning highlights ambiguous or contradictory cases for adjudication, and these cases are recycled to improve both the knowledge graph and agent policies.



Evaluation is conducted at both the module and system levels. Gold-standard supervision is drawn from published PRISMA-compliant reviews (Moher et al., 2009), providing inclusion lists, coded constructs, and synthesis narratives. Module-level metrics include F1 for NER and RE, AUROC for link prediction, recall and precision for screening, κ agreement for coding, and citation faithfulness and contradiction detection accuracy for synthesis (Yan et al., 2024; Skarlinski et al., 2024). Beyond metrics, expert panels evaluate interpretive depth, contradiction handling, and cultural relevance to ensure the system meets scholarly standards of educational review.
