TITLE: To what extent does nonanalytic reasoning contribute to visual learning in cytopathology?
AUTHORS: Andrew Evered, Darren Walker, Andrew A. Watt, Nick Perham
YEAR: 2013
DOI: 10.1002/cncy.21263

CONTENT:
To What Extent Does Nonanalytic Reasoning Contribute
to Visual Learning in Cytopathology?
Andrew Evered, BSc 1,2; Darren Walker, PhD 2; Andrew A. Watt, PhD 2; and and Nick Perham, PhD 2
BACKGROUND: Acquisition of visual interpretation skills in cytopathology may involve 2 strategies. Analytic strategies
require trainees to base their interpretive decisions on carefully considered and often exhaustive cytomorphologic feature
lists, a process that can be time-consuming and inefficient. In contrast, nonanalytic pattern recognition strategies are
rarely encouraged during training, even though this approach is characteristic of expert diagnostic behavior. This study
evaluated the potential role of nonanalytic learning in cytopathology as an efficient alternative to analytic training.
METHODS: Forty-nine cytology novice participants undertook an initial image interpretation test to obtain baseline diag-
nostic accuracy (test 1). Twenty-four participants subsequently received training in basic cervical cytomorphology and
were given a list of cell features for future reference (the “analytic” group). The remaining 25 participants were simply
shown 20 nonannotated paired images of normal and abnormal cervical cells (the “nonanalytic” group). Following a prac-
tice phase, both groups were retested (test 2). Prior to a final test (test 3), participants in both groups were instructed to
adopt a combined analytic/nonanalytic diagnostic strategy. Diagnostic accuracy and response times were measured in
each test. RESULTS: Diagnostic accuracy in both groups improved significantly between tests 1 and 2 ( P<.001) but
decreased between tests 2 and 3 ( P<.05). Speed of response to test images was generally faster under nonanalytic than
under analytic conditions. CONCLUSIONS: Nonanalytic reasoning in cytopathology image interpretation can be as accu-
rate as traditional feature-based reasoning. Encouraging trainees to adopt pattern recognition strategies may help to
expedite the acquisition of visual interpretation skills in cytopathology training programs, yet combining analytic and non-
analytic reasoning do not appear to be effective. Cancer (Cancer Cytopathol) 2013;121:329–38.

KEY WORDS: cytopathology; perceptual learning; pattern recognition; training; cognition.
The primary purpose of cytopathology is to identify cancer cells or their precursors by light microscopy. 1 The
process can be broken down into 3 separate but interdependent problems: visual search for a cellular abnormal-
ity, the recognition of one or more cells as “abnormal” in appearance, and finally, a decision as to whether any
cells are sufficiently atypical to warrant further investigation of the patient. Cutting across each facet is the ques-
tion of how cytologists develop their visual and cognitive skills with training and practice. This question is im-
portant, because visual recognition and classification of abnormal cells is a subjective and error-prone task, even
among highly skilled cytologists.
2,3 Surprisingly little is known about the nature of the visual learning process in
cytopathology, which hinders the development of evidence-based training programs. 4 Training is generally
organized such that novices first learn key cytomorphological features and subsequently perfect their skills dur-
ing a lengthy period of supervised practice.
5,6 However, there is currently no consensus on the appropriate dura-
tion and design of such programs. Evidence-based strategies that minimize costs and demands on staff time may

One possible approach to learning visual interpreta-
tion skills is the so-called nonanalytic or “pattern recog-
nition” strategy, which has been widely acknowledged in
the field of radiological image interpretation.
8 Far from
being a new idea, the theoretical basis of this form of rea-
soning came from the philosopher Michael Polanyi in the
1960s, who argued that experienced clinicians possess a
“tacit knowledge” of medicine that cannot be explicitly
expressed and is applied without conscious awareness.
9
His ideas were and still are treated with skepticism by
many clinicians, who hold the contrasting view that clini-
cal judgment is a wholly explicit process deploying ex-
haustive rules and principles.
10–12 Despite its analytic
rigor, rule-based reasoning is costly in terms of its ineffi-
ciency and high demand on working memory resources.
In contrast, rapid nonanalytic perceptual processing of
clinical scenarios appears to be a common trait among
experienced clinicians.
13–15 Moreover, several authors
convincingly argue that an analytic/nonanalytic dichot-
omy is oversimplistic and instead propose that the dual
application of such strategies is more realistic.
16 Dual pro-
cess theory, as it has become known, has found strong
empirical and theoretical support.
17–20
Research spanning more than 30 years strongly sup-
ports the notion that, regardless of the visual domain,
experts adopt more efficient visual processing and
decision-making strategies than do trainees.
21 In cognitive
psychology terms, experts tend to use strategies variably
referred to in the literature as “backward reasoning,”
“similarity-driven reasoning,” or “nonanalytic reasoning,”
in which one or only a small number of tentative diagno-
ses are made from a rapid initial and largely nonanalytic
global impression of the visual scene. This reasoning strat-
egy is highly efficient in its use of short-term memory
resources, and its adoption by experts is generously sup-
ported by research in the fields of radiology, cognitive psy-
chology, and medical decision-making.
22–27 In medical
education, training strategies that include nonanalytic rea-
soning have been shown to enhance the diagnostic per-
formance of novices.
28 It therefore seems curious why
such a tactic is not usually considered in cytopathology
training programs, which, with their emphasis on the de-
velopment of visual interpretation skills, would seem to
be an ideal candidate for such an approach. Instead, cyto-
pathology training usually involves the exhaustive applica-
tion of morphological feature lists and diagnostic “rules,”
all of which must be carefully and systematically consid-
ered before arriving at a diagnosis. The reasons for this
highly analytic strategy are unclear but probably result
from a desire to instill in students a consistent diagnostic
approach and to prevent premature closure and other cog-
nitive biases. As noted above, research in fields other than
cytopathology indicates that analytic and nonanalytic rea-
soning are probably not mutually exclusive processes. A
combination of the 2 seems to be more effective than
either tactic alone,
29–31 an approach that will be tested in
this study.
In light of the uncertainties concerning visual learn-
ing processes and optimum training strategies in cytopa-
thology, the aim of this study was to compare the effect of
rule-based (analytic) training with a nonanalytic “pattern
recognition” style of learning on the subsequent diagnos-
tic performance of novice observers. We hypothesize that
novices who adopt a pure nonanalytic reasoning strategy
can achieve levels of diagnostic accuracy equal to that
achieved using a purely analytic approach. In addition, we
propose that novices instructed to adopt a combination of
nonanalytic and analytic reasoning achieve diagnostic
accuracy greater than those instructed to emphasize either
tactic alone.
To operationalize the 2 approaches to visual learn-
ing, we have adopted what is commonly referred to as
“alternative forced choice” methodology.
32 The most
common form of this type of experiment is the 2-alterna-
tive forced choice experiment in which an observer exam-
ines 2 images simultaneously, only one of which is
abnormal. The observer indicates which image is abnor-
mal, and the data are analyzed to determine the percent-
age of correct decisions. An important drawback of
alternative forced choice experiments is their lack of eco-
logical validity: the method does not resemble an actual
clinical paradigm. However, several studies suggest that
variants of this technique can successfully enhance the vis-
ual learning process in novice dermatologists and histopa-
thologists and may therefore be a useful training tool.
33–36

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Participants
The study received approval from Cardiff Metropolitan
University School of Health Sciences Ethics Committee
and Public Health Wales Research and Development
Group. Consent was obtained from 49 first- and second-
year undergraduate psychology students from varied edu-
cational backgrounds and without previous cytopathology
experience, to take part in the study. Nine participants
were male aged 19 to 22 years (median, 21 years) and 40
were female aged 18 to 54 years (median, 21 years).
Design
Participants were divided into 2 groups (Fig. 1). Each
group was subjected to a separate training and repeat test-
ing protocol, giving a between-design repeated measures
experiment. Training method (analytic versus nonana-
lytic) was chosen as the between-group independent vari-
able, whereas pretest instruction strategy served as the
repeated measures variable. The primary dependent varia-
bles were error rate and response time. Power calculations
confirmed that the number of participants was sufficient
to detect a moderate effect size with power50.8.
Materials
One hundred static digital images of epithelial cells were
acquired from routine cervical screening samples at 3
400 original magnification using a ColourView II digital
camera (Soft Imaging System Ltd, Helperby, North York-
shire, England) attached to an Olympus BX51 optical
microscope (Olympus, Tokyo, Japan). All images were
acquired using analySIS software (Soft Imaging System,
Ltd) at a resolution of 2080 pixels 3 1544 pixels and
displayed on liquid crystal color monitors at a resolution
of 1024 pixels 3 768 pixels. All images were given a
gold standard diagnosis (normal or abnormal) by expert
consensus from 3 trained cytologists. The use of digital
images as stimuli enabled a level of standardization and
experimental efficiency that would not be achievable
using glass slide specimens. Forty images were consigned
to a training set, and the remaining images were used to
generate a 60-slide image test. Two additional 60-slide
test sets were created by first inverting and then rotating
the original images through 180 /C14. The intention of this
manipulation was to minimize memory effects between
tests. As an additional safeguard against memory effects,
images were presented in a randomized sequence in
each test.

All participants initially undertook an image interpreta-
tion test (test 1) to provide baseline diagnostic accuracy of
untrained novice observers. The test consisted of a

Analytic Training Group
Following the baseline test, 24 participants were ran-
domly allocated to receive “analytic training,” in which
they were taught the morphological features of normal
and abnormal cells by an experienced cytology trainer and
shown 20 pairs of images on a computer screen. Each
image pair comprised 1 normal and 1 abnormal cell, and
images were annotated with the relevant diagnostic fea-
tures (Fig. 2). Participants in this group were also given a
typed list of features for future reference, and a full expla-
nation of the relevant cytological terminology was pro-
vided (Table 1). The trainer answered all queries and
clarified any points of uncertainty before proceeding fur-
ther. For the practice phase, cells were again presented in
normal/abnormal pairs but were not annotated. After dis-
playing each image, participants were instructed to use the
feature list to make their diagnosis and then press a
computer key to see the correct answer. There was no ele-
ment of performance measurement at this stage; the inten-
tion was purely to facilitate the learning process.
Following the practice phase, participants were given
2 separate image interpretation tests, each comprising 60
individual cell images presented in sequence (tests 2 and 3
in Fig. 1). As in test 1, these tests consisted of an equal mix
of normal and abnormal cells presented in random order.
Before test 2, participants were instructed to base their di-
agnosis solely on an analysis of the cytological features
observed. Participants were specifically instructed not to
trust any sense of familiarity with previous images and to
use only the analytic reasoning skills acquired during the
training phase. Before proceeding to test 3, instructions

Nonanalytic Training Group
This group of 25 participants was subjected to the same
format of training, practice, and testing as was the
“analytic training” group, and the same images were used.
However, no feature-driven training was provided, and
the only image annotation used during training was re-
stricted to the labels “normal” and “abnormal” (Fig. 3).
The intention, therefore, was for participants to develop
nonanalytic pattern recognition skills without assistance
from the trainer.
Following the practice phase, participants under-
took test 2, for which they were asked to provide their first
impression of each image based on their similarity to
images seen during training and practice. Following test
2, participants were asked to write a list of features they
felt were helpful in making their diagnostic decisions. Im-
mediately preceding the final test (test 3), participants in
this group were instructed to supplement their nonana-
lytic reasoning strategy with an analytical approach based
on each individual’s personal list of diagnostic features,
and to amend their initial impression as necessary.
In addition to deciding “normal” or “abnormal” for
each test image, participants in both groups provided a
confidence rating on a 1-to-5 ordinal scale using a
computer keyboard. To test the efficiency of the decision-
making process, the time to respond to each test image
was recorded automatically using DMDX software.

RESULTS
We present error rate and response time data as the main
dependent variables. In addition, we report the signal
detection variables d
0 and criterion, both of which are
derived from error rate data. Briefly, d 0 provides a mea-
sure of discriminability between “signal” and “noise,” in
this case between abnormal and normal cells. An increase
in the value of d
0 indicates improved discriminability and
better performance. 38 Criterion can be viewed as a mea-
sure of the tendency to report a cell as abnormal. A low
value indicates a liberal criterion, whereas higher values are
more indicative of a conservative criterion. The conver-
sion of error data to d
0 and criterion therefore helps to elu-
cidate whether changes in error rates are due to alterations
in discriminability or changes in diagnostic threshold, or
both. Furthermore, unlike error rates, d
0 and criterion are
normally distributed, permitting parametric statistical
analysis. The results were evaluated by 2-way mixed
design analysis of variance using the statistical package
SPSS (version 19; SPSS Inc, Chicago, Ill). Two-sided
P values of <.05 were considered to be statistically
significant.

DISCUSSION
This purpose of this study was to provide an insight into
the nature of visual learning in cytopathology, for which
there is a dearth of literature. Research in other fields sug-
gests that analytic and nonanalytic strategies play a com-
bined role, but the notion that nonanalytic “pattern
recognition” can be operationalized as a teaching and
learning strategy is not popular.
40 Indeed, most discus-
sions relating to medical decision-making acknowledge
the difficulty in incorporating pattern recognition as a
diagnostic strategy in training programs, referring to this
aspect of learning as the “hidden curriculum.”
41,42 Cytol-
ogists, similar to many other clinicians who rely on their
visual skills to make complex diagnoses, accept that at
least a portion of their diagnostic armamentarium comes
from a tacit recognition of cellular patterns, and whole
textbooks have been devoted to the subject.
43 However,
there is currently a lack of empirical research into the na-
ture of perceptual learning in cytopathology, which our
study begins to address.
By recruiting participants with no previous experi-
ence in cytopathology and examining their diagnostic per-
formance under controlled laboratory conditions, we
were able to demonstrate a significant element of nonana-
lytic reasoning from the earliest learning stages. After only
brief exposure to paired images of normal and abnormal
cells in the style of alternative forced choice training, the
diagnostic performance of participants improved signifi-
cantly in subsequent tests. The improvement took the
form of a decline in miss errors (ie, false negatives) with-
out any noticeable increases in false alarms (ie, false posi-
tives). The increase in d
0 and decrease in criterion between
tests 1 and 2 are consistent with improvements in the abil-
ity of participants to discriminate between normal and
abnormal cells and a corresponding shift to a more liberal
threshold for reporting images as abnormal. This pattern
of results is not entirely unexpected. When participants
become aware of the features of cancer cells, discrimina-
tion should improve alongside a greater readiness to
report cellular abnormalities. The nonsignificant upward
criterion shifts between tests 2 and 3 perhaps indicate a sta-
bilization of decision threshold as participants became
increasingly confident in their diagnostic abilities.
What is perhaps more surprising in this study is that
improvements in diagnostic performance did not require
intensive rule-based training. Overall, the diagnostic
performance of participants was no better following rule-
based training than after a brief period of nonanalytic pat-
tern recognition training, thus supporting the hypothesis
that a nonanalytic reasoning strategy can achieve levels of
diagnostic accuracy equal to that achieved using a purely
analytic approach. Moreover, response time data suggest
that nonanalytic strategies are more efficient than rule-
based training. This finding is encouraging because
rule-based training in cytopathology is time-consuming
and labor-intensive. Given the noninferior performance
of nonanalytic strategies, their incorporation into cytology
training and skill maintenance programs might be of tre-
mendous benefit to cytopathology services and could
shorten training times considerably. In the United King-
dom, for instance, nonmedical trainees are mandated to

Interestingly, the idea of developing trainees’ perceptual
learning skills is not new outside the domain of cytopa-
thology. Effective perceptual learning technology has
been developed in fields as diverse as airplane navigation,
speech and language therapy, surgical training, and
mathematics.
44
The deterioration in diagnostic performance
between tests 2 and 3 under both analytic and nonanalytic
training conditions, although disappointing, is worthy of
discussion. Clinical reasoning theory predicts an
improved performance when observers are instructed to
combine analytic and nonanalytic reasoning strategies,
15
but participants in both training groups showed a signifi-
cant decline in d
0 in the final test. The hypothesis that a
combined nonanalytic/analytic reasoning strategy can
achieve diagnostic accuracy greater than that employing
either method alone is therefore not supported. The possi-
bility that participant boredom and fatigue contributed to
the decline in performance during the final test should be
considered, and future experiments should attempt to
control for these factors. An alternative explanation for
the relatively poor performance in the final test, which
occurred in both groups of participants, is the possibility
that participants were confused by the instructions given
just prior to the test. We readily acknowledge that the
meaning and validity of an instruction to “combine” ana-
lytic and nonanalytic reasoning is not entirely clear and
that practitioners are unlikely to consciously do this in
practice. This explanation must, of course, remain specu-
lative until further studies are undertaken.
Although the focus of this study was on diagnostic
accuracy, we took the opportunity to gather response time
data in an attempt to gauge the efficiency of each training
modality. Although the interpretation and meaning of
response times is uncertain, several investigators propose
that short response times (generally <1 second), at least
among experts, indicate rapid nonanalytic cognitive proc-
essing.
45,46 The results of this study support the notion
that novices can also respond very quickly to complex vis-
ual scenes, with generally quicker response times noted
under nonanalytic reasoning conditions than under ana-
lytic conditions. Importantly, the fast response times do
not appear to be at the expense of diagnostic accuracy.
This study investigated the acquisition of visual
interpretation skills, which is only one of the skills
required of a trained cytologist. Equally important in
cytopathology training is the development of accurate lo-
cator skills, because slides are scanned at low magnifica-
tion. Studying the visual scanning skills of microscopists
using an eye-tracking device has been attempted previ-
ously, but is hampered by the physical size of the eye-
tracker and the close working distance imposed by a
standard light microscope.
47,48 However, the increasing
availability of virtual microscopy, whereby digital images
of whole glass slide specimens are displayed on a computer
monitor, permits the separation of observer from the clin-
ical image and provides an exciting opportunity to study
this important determinant of cytologist proficiency.
49
The results of this study generally support a long his-
tory of theories generated mainly in the cognitive psychol-
ogy and radiology literature that put rapid visual
perceptual processing at the heart of expert performance
and how it develops.
50–53 Our data fit well with such the-
ories, and for the first time we demonstrate that cytopa-
thology has much in common with radiology with regard
