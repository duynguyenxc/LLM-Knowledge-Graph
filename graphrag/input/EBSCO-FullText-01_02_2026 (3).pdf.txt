Abstract
Background: Diagnostic error can be caused by several types of cognitive bias, which may be reversed by enhancing analytic
reasoning.
Aims: To evaluate whether enhancing analytic reasoning can increase diagnostic accuracy in objective structured clinical
examination (OSCE) in medical students.
Methods: All fourth-year medical students, randomly assigned to the analytic reasoning or control groups, undertook the OSCE
with four cases using standardized patients. The analytic reasoning group was requested to list differential diagnoses and findings
compatible or not compatible with each diagnosis prior to providing a diagnosis, while the control group provided a diagnosis
without these processes. Mean diagnostic accuracy scores ( perfect score, 4.0) from four cases of OSCE were compared between
the two groups.
Results: One hundred forty-five students were randomly assigned to the analytic reasoning group ( n ¼ 65) or the control group
(n ¼ 80). The baseline characteristics, including grade point average and the scores from each patient encounter, were comparable
between groups. Mean diagnostic accuracy scores were significantly higher in the analytic reasoning group than in the control
group (3.40 /C6 0.66 versus 3.05 /C6 0.98; p ¼ 0.011).
Conclusion: Enhancement of analytic reasoning may improve diagnostic accuracy in novice doctors.
Introduction
Diagnostic errors comprise a large proportion of preventable
medical errors and can be a result of numerous types of
cognitive bias (Graber et al. 2005). Common cognitive bias
include premature closure (‘‘the tendency to stop considering
other possibilities after reaching a diagnosis’’), anchoring (‘‘the
tendency to perceptually lock onto salient features too early in
the diagnostic process’’), and confirmation bias (‘‘the tendency
to seek data to confirm, not refute, the hypothesis’’) (Croskerry
2003).
Analytic reasoning, one method used to combat bias, is a
reflective approach that involves the consideration of alterna-
tive diagnoses and of evidence to refute a diagnosis as well as
to support it (Norman & Eva 2010). However, there are few
studies evaluating the effect of analytic reasoning on reducing
diagnostic error (Mamede et al. 2010a, b). We conducted a
randomized controlled study to determine whether the
enhancement of analytic reasoning increases medical students’
diagnostic accuracy on the objective structured clinical exam-
ination (OSCE) using standardized patients.
Methods
This randomized control study was conducted in 2011 at Seoul
National University College of Medicine (Seoul, South Korea).
All fourth-year medical students participated in the OSCE ,
which consists of four cases (IgA nephropathy, neurogenic
diabetes insipidus, acute pyelonephritis, and exercise-induced
asthma) developed by the Seoul Kyeonggi CPX Consortium; it
was administered to nine examination groups of students over
3 days. Each case required 16 min to administer, with 1 min to
introduce the next case, 10 min for the student–standardized
patient encounter, and 5 min after the encounter for the
student to report the most probable diagnosis (Park et al.
2007).
The students were randomly assigned to nine examination
groups through a computerized random selection process.
Each examination group was also randomly assigned to either
the analytic reasoning group or the control group. After
encountering patients, students in the analytic reasoning group
received an answer sheet containing a table designed to
enhance analytic reasoning. Students in the analytic reasoning
group were asked to complete the table with differential
diagnoses and symptoms or signs compatible with or differing
from each diagnosis, and then provide one most probable
diagnosis. Students in the control group were simply asked to
provide one most probable diagnosis on an answer sheet that
lacked the table provided to the test group. Each group was
given 5 min to complete their respective answer sheets;
students in the reflective reasoning group had to complete
the table and provide a diagnosis within 5 min. Before and

The mean diagnostic accuracy scores ( perfect score, 4.0)

Student’s t-test was used to compare age, grade point average,
OSCE scores and the chi-squared test to compare sex and

performed using SPSS software (version 19.0; SPSS Inc.,

board provided study approval.
Results
A total of 145 fourth-year students were randomly assigned to
the analytic reasoning group ( n ¼ 65) or the control group
(n ¼ 80). Baseline characteristics, including grade point aver-
age, were comparable between groups (Table 1). OSCE scores
from encounters with standardized patients did not differ
significantly between the two groups.
Mean diagnostic accuracy scores were significantly higher
in the analytic reasoning group than in the control group
(3.40 /C6 0.66 versus 3.05 /C6 0.98; 95% CI of difference, 0.08–
0.62; p ¼ 0.016). In the analytic reasoning group, diagnostic
accuracy did not change significantly with respect to case
order ( p ¼ 0.93 for trend).
Discussion
While most previous studies evaluating the effect of analytic
reasoning on diagnostic accuracy used written examinations
(Mamede et al. 2008, 2010b), this study employed the OSCE
using standardized patients, which may better reflect real-life
clinical situations (Barrows 1993). In addition, this study did
not compel any experimental, artificial condition prohibiting
analytic reasoning to the control group. Instead, we evaluated
the value of enhancing analytic reasoning compared with
routine, usual reasoning in medical students.
The finding that scores from the patient encounters were
comparable between the two groups implies that students in
both groups obtained similar clinical information from the
standardized patients. Nonetheless, diagnostic accuracy was
significantly higher in the analytic reasoning group than in the
control group, suggesting that using an apparatus (in this case,
the table) to enhance analytic reasoning can help reduce
diagnostic error in clinical reasoning. Completing the table
presented on the answer sheet forced the consideration of
alternative diagnoses and of findings that conflicted with a
suspected diagnosis. These methods, in combination with the
analysis of compatible findings, might contribute to reducing
cognitive bias. These findings suggest that the training and
practice of analytic reasoning should be emphasized to
medical students and novice doctors.
This study has a few limitations. First, this study was
performed among fourth-year medical students just before
attaining the license of medical doctor. Therefore, these results
may not be generalizable to experienced doctors. However,
senior residents may be more susceptible to cognitive error
(e.g., availability bias) than junior residents, which can be
corrected with analytic reasoning (Mamede et al. 2010b).
Second, the effect of analytic reasoning on diagnostic accuracy
can be case specific. In this study, diagnostic accuracy
regarding the case of acute pyelonephritis was similar between
the groups. Because the clinical presentation of this case was
typical, students who took a heuristic ( pattern recognition)
approach may not have made diagnostic errors. Another study
has demonstrated contradictory results, suggesting that ana-
lytic reasoning exercised by medical students was ineffective
in complex cases, but effective in simple cases (Mamede et al.
2010a). That study’s findings suggest that analytic reasoning
may not be helpful if a case is too complex with respect to the
level of students’ medical knowledge.
In conclusion, this randomized controlled study demon-
strated that, among fourth-year medical students diagnosing
standardized patients, the enhancement of analytic reasoning
