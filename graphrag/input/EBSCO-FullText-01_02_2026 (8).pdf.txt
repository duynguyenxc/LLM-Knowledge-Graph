CONTEXT Recent studies suggest that self-expla-
nation (SE) while diagnosing cases fosters the
development of clinical reasoning in medical
students; however, the conditions that optimise the
impact of SE remain unknown. The example-based
learning framework justiﬁes an exploration of
students’ use of their own SEs combined with the
study of examples. This study aimed to assess the
impact on medical students’ diagnostic perfor-
mance of: (i) combining students’ SEs with their
listening to examples of residents’ SEs, and (ii)
the addition of prompts (speciﬁc questions) while
working with examples.
METHODS This study consisted of a training
phase and an assessment phase conducted 1 week
later. In the training phase, 54 Year 3 medical
students were randomly assigned to one of three
groups. In all groups, students ﬁrst solved four
clinical cases using SE. Subsequently, Group 1
listened to examples of residents’ SEs with
prompts; Group 2 listened to examples of resi-
dents’ SEs without prompts, and the control group
solved word puzzles. Then, all students again
solved the same four cases. One week later, all
students solved four similar and four different
cases. Students’ diagnostic performance and
diagnostic accuracy scores were assessed for each
case at each time-point.
RESULTS Although all groups’ diagnostic accu-
racy scores on similar cases improved signiﬁcantly
between the training and the assessment phase,
Group 1 showed a signiﬁcantly higher diagnostic
performance score after 1 week than the control
group (p = 0.037). On different cases, Group 1
obtained signiﬁcantly higher diagnostic accuracy
(p = 0.011) and diagnostic performance
(p < 0.001) scores than the control group and a
signiﬁcantly higher diagnostic performance score
than Group 2 (p = 0.018).
CONCLUSIONS Self-explanation seems to be an
effective technique to help medical students learn
clinical reasoning. Its impact is increased signiﬁ-
cantly by combining it with examples of residents’
SEs and prompts. Although students’ exposure to
examples of clinical reasoning is important, their
‘active processing’ of these examples appears to be
critical to their learning from them.

INTRODUCTION
Although clinical reasoning is viewed as a complex,
multidimensional and integrated skill, a well-organ-
ised medical knowledge base remains at its core.
1,2
One of the major foci of clinical education is to
expand, revise and reﬁne this knowledge base, in
large part through the process of clinical reasoning
in response to patients’ problems.
1–3 To learn more
from these encounters, students should actively
reﬂect on the clinical problems.
3 Little is known
about instructional strategies that teachers can use
to guide students’ practice with problems.
3,4 The
use of self-explanation (SE) in the course of solving
clinical cases has been shown to be effective for
medical students at the clerkship level (the last seg-
ment of the undergraduate curriculum, which con-
sists of successive clinical rotations over periods of
12–24 months depending on the programme) and
seems to be a promising technique to support the
learning of clinical reasoning in context.
5 In addi-
tion, recent studies have highlighted the importance
of interacting with and observing peers (students,
residents) and expert clinicians for the improve-
ment of residents’ and clinicians’ reasoning in prac-
tice.
6,7 Combining SE with modelling by residents
might support students’ clinical reasoning. Resi-
dents have been recognised as important role mod-
els in the education of medical students.
8 However,
little is known about their speciﬁc cognitive model-
ling or contribution to the development of students’
clinical reasoning. Clinical reasoning can be viewed
through different theoretical perspectives. In the
present paper, we use the cognitive sciences frame
of reference to explore the potential beneﬁts of SE
and active or prompted observation of residents’
models of SE on students’ diagnostic performance
in clerkship.
Self-explanation is an active learning process that
consists of generating explanations to oneself when
working through learning materials.
9–11 While self-
explaining, learners engage in knowledge elabora-
tion, integration and monitoring, allowing them to
revise their knowledge and build a more coherent
representation that facilitates the transfer of learn-
ing.
9 The beneﬁt of SE on learning has been dem-
onstrated in various domains. 9 More speciﬁcally in
medical education, it has been found that, in the
solving of paper cases, clerks using SE improved
their diagnostic performance on new cases in the
same clinical area 1 week later compared with stu-
dents who did not use SE.
5 As knowledge restructur-
ing and the emergence of illness scripts are critical
to the development of clinical reasoning in stu-
dents,2 it has been proposed that generating SE
during the process of reasoning through and diag-
nosing cases may foster the development of better
organised and richer illness scripts used for clinical
reasoning.
5 The beneﬁcial effect was observed only
with unfamiliar and not with familiar cases. Unfamil-
iar cases seem to encourage students to reactivate
biomedical knowledge, potentially creating new
links between biomedical and clinical knowledge,
thus leading to a more coherent mental or cognitive
representation of diseases.
12,13
The use of prompts, which are speciﬁc instructions
that require learners to process the example or the
content in a speciﬁc way, has been shown to
enhance the effect of SE on learning
14,15 ‘Justiﬁca-
tion prompts’ require students to generate the prin-
cipal justiﬁcation and to focus on the underlying
concepts. Examples of this type of prompt are: What
principle is being applied here? This particular choice is
correct because ... Transposed to clinical reasoning,
this type of prompt may facilitate the activation of
biomedical knowledge, such as pathophysiological
mechanisms, while solving the problem, potentially
leading to new links with clinical knowledge. By
contrast, ‘mental model revision prompts’ help stu-
dents to highlight discrepancies between their
knowledge representation and the observed model.
Examples of this type of prompt are: How does it
relate to what you already know? Does it help you gain
more insight into how to solve the problem?
15 When
applied to clinical reasoning, the student’s compar-
ing and contrasting of his or her own mental
representation of diseases with an example from a
colleague might assist in that student’s revision of
knowledge.
Observing models has also been shown to foster the
construction of mental representations in general.
16
Otherwise, for clinical reasoning, it has been sug-
gested that when reasoning is made explicit in clini-
cal practice, it not only becomes exposed to self-
critique and to feedback from others, but it also
becomes available to others as a potentially useful
example from which to learn.
6 The process of learn-
ing or acquiring a skill by observing examples or
models of that skill is called ‘example-based learn-
ing’ (EBL).
16 It has been suggested that EBL pro-
motes the construction of appropriate cognitive
representations that guide later performance. Exam-
ple-based learning has been used for a wide range
of tasks from psychomotor skills to highly and less
structured cognitive skills.
16 Hence, EBL may be
relevant for learning clinical reasoning. According

to the EBL framework, the skill in the example
must ﬁrst be ‘salient’ and ‘visible’ for the learner.
Secondly, the difference between the model’s and
the learner’s level of expertise is also worth consid-
ering.
16,17 A relatively small gap in expertise separat-
ing the model from the learner may support the
learner’s self-efﬁcacy (the learner’s judgement of his
or her capacity to attain the observed performance)
and clarify outcome expectations. It may also pro-
vide social (i.e. feeling at ease) and cognitive con-
gruence.
18,19 By contrast, an expert model, which is
largely automatised and works at a higher level of
abstraction, may be difﬁcult for students to follow.
Thirdly, to optimise learning when working with
examples, students should engage in cognitive activi-
ties that may occur before, during or after the
observation.
A recent study investigated the added value of lis-
tening to peers’ (clerks) or experts’ SEs to the
effect of SE on medical students’ learning of clini-
cal diagnosis (M. Chamberland, S. Mamede, C. St-
Onge, J. Setrakian and H. Schmidt, Universit /C19e de
Sherbrooke, unpublished study 2014). In compari-
son with a control group, only a small beneﬁt of
exposure to peer or expert SE was found when
the study subjects were tested on the same cases
1 week later and no effect was found when they
were tested on different and more difﬁcult cases.
The authors suggested that using examples
sourced from individuals with an intermediate
level of expertise, such as residents (rather than
peers or experts), and prompting students while
they listened might have produced better out-
comes. These ideas were tested in the present
study, the purpose of which was to assess the
impact on medical students’ learning of clinical
diagnosis of: (i) combining students’ SE with lis-
tening to examples of residents’ SEs, and (ii) add-
ing prompts as the students worked with the
examples of SE.
METHODS
Design
A mixed design (between and within design) was
used for this experimental study, which comprised
two phases, a training phase and an assessment
phase run 1 week later. In the training phase, par-
ticipants were randomly assigned to one of three
groups (Fig. 1). In each group, the students ﬁrst
solved four clinical cases using SE. Then, Group 1
listened to examples of residents’ SEs for the same
cases with prompts, Group 2 listened to examples of
residents’ SEs without prompts, and the control
group solved word puzzles. Subsequently, all stu-
dents solved the same four cases again. One week
later, all students solved four similar cases (near-
transfer cases) and four different cases (far-transfer
cases).
Students’ diagnostic performance was assessed on
each case and at each performance moment.
Figure 1 illustrates the study design.
Participants
Participants were Year 3 medical students on the
University of Sherbrooke undergraduate pro-
gramme, which consists of 2.5 years of problem-
based learning followed by 18 months of clerkship.
At the time of the study, participants were engaged
in their ﬁrst 8 months of clerkship. All students on
clinical rotations within the Departments of Medi-
cine and Paediatrics during June to August 2013
were invited to participate in the study. Participa-
tion was voluntary and no ﬁnancial incentive was
offered. Written informed consent was obtained.
Of the 60 students invited to participate, 58
accepted and were randomly assigned to one of
three groups: Group 1, in which students were
exposed to examples of residents’ SEs with
prompts; Group 2, in which students were exposed
to examples of residents’ SEs without prompts, and
a control group. Fig. 1 gives further details. Data
were analysed for the 54 students who completed
the two phases of the study (Group 1, n = 19;
Group 2, n = 18; control group, n = 17).
Ethics approval was obtained from the Ethics Com-
mittee for Research, Education and Social Sciences
at the University of Sherbrooke.
Materials
Clinical cases
Twelve clinical cases were used for the study. All cases
were related to jaundice because this clinical topic
was rather unfamiliar to the Year 3 medical students
involved.
5,12 The cases consisted of four training
cases, four near-transfer cases and four far-transfer
cases. Case descriptions were approximately 300
words long and included the chief complaint, back-
ground information, and ﬁndings from history tak-
ing, physical examination and laboratory tests. The
training cases and far-transfer cases had been

Self-explanation examples and clinical reasoning
developed and used in previous studies. 5,12 Near-
transfer cases were developed and revised by two
senior internists/clinical teachers (MC, JS) for this
study.
The topic of jaundice was broken down into the fol-
lowing four broad disease categories according to
pathophysiological mechanism: acute hepatitis;
chronic liver disease; obstructive jaundice, and
haemolysis. Each set of four cases included one case
in each of the four categories. The near-transfer
cases were similar to the training cases and used the
same key elements and ﬁnal diagnoses. Only superﬁ-
cial data irrelevant to the diagnosis were changed
from the initial cases (e.g. gender, age, background
information). Far-transfer cases, however, presented
different clinical scenarios and different ﬁnal
diagnoses (Table 1). The latter were also made more
difﬁcult by a less typical presentation, less common
ﬁnal diagnosis or the presence of comorbidity.
In the training phase, cases were presented in a
booklet with one case per page. Cases were
displayed in a particular order in half of the
booklets and in the reverse order in the other
half. These two different booklets were distributed
alternately to recruited students. In the assessment
phase, for transfer cases, we used a similar strategy
to assemble two differently ordered booklets in
which we intercalated four ﬁller cases in other
clinical areas.
Examples of residents’ SEs
To develop the examples of residents’ SEs, three-
second-year internal medicine residents from the
University of Sherbrooke were recruited on a volun-
tary basis. Compared with the participants, the resi-
dents had two additional years of training. After a
20-minute introduction to SE similar to the proce-
dure used with students in the study, the residents
were asked to self-explain while reasoning through
each of the four training cases, but not to provide
their ﬁnal diagnostic opinion at the end. The SEs
were audio-recorded electronically and anonymised.

Prompts
Two different types of prompt, justiﬁcation
prompts (e.g. What principle is being applied here? Do
you agree with this interpretation? Explain; How do you
justify this interpretation? What pathophysiological
mechanisms could underline this interpretation? ) and
mental model revision prompts (e.g. How does this
reasoning relate to yours? How does this interpretation
compare with what you already know? How does this
analysis help you gain more insight into how to solve
the problem? What new information does this provide
for you? ) were designed in order to support
students’ active listening to the examples of
residents’ SEs.
A total of 12 prompts, six of each type, were
incorporated into the four resident SE recordings.
Hence, for each resident SE recording, three
prompts were inserted at different moments in
the clinical reasoning process. After each prompt,
a 35-second sequence of silence was incorporated
into the sound track in order to provide students
with strictly controlled time in which to respond
to the prompt. To keep time on task equal for
students listening to examples of resident SE with-
out prompts, similar sequences of silence were
added at the same moments in the resident SE
recordings.
We pilot-tested the materials with three medical stu-
dents who were not involved in the study, and made
minor adjustments to ensure the clarity of materials
and instructions.
Procedure
Training phase
In this phase, a research assistant met each
participant individually. After a brief introduction
to SE, the technique was illustrated to the student
with a 7-minute audio example of SE on a clinical
case not used in the study (mono-arthritis). Then,
the participant was asked to solve the ﬁrst train-
ing case, while self-explaining aloud for 8 minutes.
He or she was then given 2 minutes to write
down the answers to three questions:
Q1 What is the most likely diagnosis?
Q2 What are the two main arguments supporting
this diagnosis?
Q3 List two plausible alternative diagnoses.
Participants in Group 1 then listened to the 8-min-
ute example of resident SE on the same training
case. After each prompt, the student was given
35 seconds in which to verbalise his or her answers
to the prompt. At the end of the recording, the stu-
dent was again given 2 minutes to answer the same
three questions regarding the diagnosis.
Participants in Group 2 listened to the 8-minute
example of resident SE without prompts. They were
advised that a few silent sequences were included in
the sound track, but were not given further speciﬁc
instructions. They were asked to remain silent dur-
ing the sequences. They were then allowed 2 min-
utes to answer the three questions.
Participants in the control group were invited after
the case to move to the next page of their booklet
and to solve a word puzzle for 8 minutes. This task
was intended to equate time on task and to avoid
students engaging in additional SE about the case.
20
Then, they were allowed 2 minutes to answer the
three questions.
This procedure was repeated for all four training
cases.
At the end of the session, the research assistant
asked the student open-ended questions on his or
her perceived learning experiences with the
approaches used in the session.
The training phase lasted for 2 hours for each
group and was audio-recorded.
Assessment phase
One week later, participants from all groups gath-
ered for the 2-hour assessment phase supervised by
a research assistant. Participants were requested to
solve in silence 12 cases presented in a booklet:
four near-transfer cases; four far-transfer cases, and

four intercalated ﬁllers. For each case, they
answered the same three questions on their diagno-
ses. At the end of the session, participants com-
pleted a brief questionnaire asking for their age,
gender, clinical rotation, and any personal work or
teaching activities related to the topic of jaundice
that had occurred during the preceding week.
There was no difference between the groups for
any of these items (Table 2).
Two research assistants, who had participated in a
standardisation exercise, ran all the individual and
group sessions according to written protocols.
Data analysis
For all cases (training, near-transfer and far-transfer
cases) and all three questions, each participant’s
answer was scored on a 3-point scale (0 = incorrect,
1 = partially correct, 2 = entirely correct). For the
training cases and the far-transfer cases, partici-
pants’ answers to the three questions on diagnosis
were assessed using a scoring grid developed and
validated in a previous study,
5 and all responses
were scored by one rater (MC), who was blind to
the participants’ conditions. In the previous study,
scoring of the cases by two raters had shown inter-
rater reliability, measured by intraclass correlations
ranging from 0.70 to 0.95. Responses on the near-
transfer cases, which had not been used previously,
were scored independently by two raters (MC and
JS) who were unaware of the conditions under
which they had been given. Inter-rater reliability,
measured by intraclass correlations, ranged from
0.69 to 1.00. Disagreements were resolved by
discussion.
Two measurements were derived from participants’
answers: the diagnostic accuracy score (the score on
Q1), and the diagnostic performance score (the
sum of scores on Q1, Q2 and Q3), which reﬂected,
respectively, the accuracy of the diagnosis and the
quality of diagnostic reasoning. The scores on each
set of four cases were summed and then expressed
as a percentage of the total score.

DISCUSSION
This experimental study with SE in medical clerks
explored the added value of listening to an example
of a resident’s SE with or without prompts.
The results ﬁrst demonstrate that all groups,
including the control group (SE alone), improved
signiﬁcantly in both diagnostic accuracy and diag-
nostic performance scores on near-transfer cases.
This supports the growing evidence that this tech-
nique, even without external feedback, is effective
for learning clinical reasoning at clerkship level.

represent near peers for medical clerks. Their

8,18 At the same time, the
residents’ more developed reasoning acts as a
source of new information from which students
can learn. At the end of the session, the research
assistant verbally asked the students open-ended
questions on their perceived learning experience
with the techniques used in the session. The role
of these techniques in their learning was highly
appreciated by students in this study. Students
reported that listening to residents’ examples
helped them to elaborate further on their own
ideas. They also reported that it helped them to
conﬁrm their reasoning and to complete their
ﬁnal diagnostic opinion and argument with some
ideas they had not raised by themselves.
Furthermore, students must be cognitively engaged
in order to gain from working with even the best
and most suitable example. In our study, students’
SE was used as a preparatory task and was
intended to prepare the students cognitively for
the example that followed. The incorporation of
only three general prompts to each of the four
examples of resident SE interestingly had an
additional impact. Justiﬁcation and mental model
revision prompts presumably engaged students in
additional SE while they listened to the example,
thereby supporting the deep processing of the
resident SE. This ﬁnding further reinforces the
importance of stimulating students to reactivate
underlying pathophysiological mechanisms when
trying to make sense of information while solv-
ing unfamiliar cases.
12,22 The signiﬁcant difference
observed between Groups 1 and 2 in diagnostic
performance scores on far-transfer cases
further emphasises the important role played by
prompts.
Interestingly, the dynamics of improvement on
near-transfer cases in the control group with SE
alone and in the two other groups, who were
exposed to example SEs, differed. For the latter two
groups, the improvement was immediate and main-
tained over time, whereas the group subjected to SE
alone took 1 week to appreciate the full impact,
which again suggests deeper data processing in the
course of a week. Other studies have shown this
delayed effect of SE or structured reﬂection on
medical students’ diagnostic reasoning (M. Cham-
berland, S. Mamede, C. St-Onge, J. Setrakian and
H. Schmidt, Universit /C19e de Sherbrooke, unpublished
study 2014).
20 Listening to models seemed to accel-
erate the effect, although there appeared to be a
ceiling on further improvement. However, the
scores obtained after the intervention in the train-
ing phase were very high, which left no room for
extra gain. Perhaps an additional improvement after
1 week might be observed if students were to work
with more difﬁcult cases in the training phase.
This study has limitations. As it was conducted
under well-controlled conditions, the generalisation
of its ﬁndings to real and busy clinical training envi-
ronments may be restricted. Participants were Year 3
medical students and the same results may not
apply to learners at other levels of training.
This study has implications for practice. Self-expla-
nation is an easy, inexpensive and effective way of
ensuring students are cognitively engaged as they
solve cases in clerkship. Moreover, SE seems to be a
useful technique for making reasoning explicit and
available for learning by colleagues, especially near
peers. Natural examples of clinical reasoning,
although not didactically designed, are useful and
may be made available in the clinical learning envi-
ronment without too much preparation, even with-
out the intervention of clinical teachers. Students’
exposure to adapted examples of clinical reasoning
is important, but their active processing of these
examples seems critical. Prompts used in this study
were general and could be applied to any clinical
problem.
Future studies might aim to transpose these ideas to
concrete activities in real clinical situations. The
impact of similar techniques in other tandems of
near peers, such as senior residents and clinical
teachers, also warrants further investigation.
